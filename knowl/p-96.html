They key that makes this argument work is the logical form of the statements involved and our agreement about the relationship between those forms and truth. The first statement has the form “\(P\) or \(Q\)”, which in symbols we write \(P \vee Q\text{.}\) We set rules for the conditions under which this disjunction (i.e. “or” statement) is true, depending only on the truth values of \(P\) and \(Q\text{.}\) Similarly, the second statement has the form \(P \imp Q\text{,}\) which is true precisely when \(P\) is false or \(Q\) is true (we will convince ourselves this is the right choice later).
